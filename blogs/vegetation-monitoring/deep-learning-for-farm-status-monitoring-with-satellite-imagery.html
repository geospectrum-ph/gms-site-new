Deep Learning for Farm Status Monitoring With Satellite Imagery

Geospectrum Remote Sensing | March 01, 2024

READ TIME: 10 MINUTES


Vegetation Monitoring for Farms

Vegetation monitoring from remote sensing is the process of extracting vegetation information by interpreting satellite images based on the analysis of various elements such as the image color, texture, tone, pattern, and association information (Xie et al., 2008). Diverse methods have been developed and introduced. One widely used method to study vegetation dynamics is through vegetation indices (VIs). Vegetation indices mainly use remotely sensed vegetation properties such as photosynthetic activity and canopy structure. The best-known vegetation index is the Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), which is considered the standard for vegetation monitoring. It takes advantage of the differences in the health status of the plants (Zeng et al., 2020). It utilizes the different solar radiation absorption phenomena of green plants in the red spectral and near-infrared spectral regions (Ayhan et al., 2020). It is an indispensable index that is commonly used in farming. NDVI values ranging from 0 to 1 are commonly used to build thresholds that serve as crop status indicators. A densely vegetated healthy crop will typically have high NDVI values (e.g., 0.7 to 1.0), and this information can serve as a sign of harvest. Once harvested, the area will have low NDVI values due to the lack of vegetation. VI-based findings, however, can vary between indices, sensors, quality control measures, compositing algorithms, and corrections for atmospheric and sun-target-sensor geometry (Zeng et al., 2022).

An NDVI map of irrigated fields in Kherson. Green indicates high NDVI and light brown indicates low NDVI. Dark green circles are easily viewable and show dense vegetation. Source: Tomchenko Olha, Wikimedia.


Deep learning is an alternative method that has found a wide range of applications in remote sensing after several breakthroughs have been reported in a variety of computer vision tasks. These tasks include image classification, object detection, and tracking, and semantic segmentation. Compared to traditional approaches, deep-learning-based methods provide an end-to-end solution and show better performance than traditional pixel-based methods, which often produce undesired effects such as salt-and-pepper noise (Ayhan et al., 2020).

Here, we developed a deep learning-based solution that can help monitor fields using PlanetScope imagery. We used sugarcane farming in Negros Island as our test case.


Sugarcane Farming in Negros Island

Negros Island is commonly known as the sugar capital of the Philippines and contributes more than half of the total sugar production in the country (Villones, 2019). It is cited as the most productive of the sugarcane farms in the country, yielding a low of 62.37 tons of cane per hectare and up to a high of 72.92 tons (SRA, 2015). There’s no established planting season, although there have been recommendations to shift the planting season from May to March to achieve a 13% increase in yield (WWF-Philippines, 2019). Based on the calendars published by the Sugar Regulatory Administration, the milling season typically starts in September and ends in April the following year. After harvesting, the remaining residue is often burned in preparation for the next planting season (Tongol, 2019). There’s little information on sugarcane monitoring systems in the province at the farm or plot level, and this serves as a good case to test a large-scale satellite-based solution.


Deep Learning with Satellite Imagery

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that attempts to model how we learn. In the context of satellite imagery, deep learning finds patterns to recognize a particular class or object. Multiple dimensions are incorporated, including textures, edges, shapes, and even colors. For this, we tested the suitability of deep learning to monitor field plots. We settled on using the technology to classify pixels into classes or categories to provide plot status. Some of the most common information needed on a farm is its availability, such as the presence of vegetation or its harvest. This is easy to manage on a small farm but may be costly on large-scale farms. In this area, satellite imagery can supplement information that would typically take days if done manually.

After analyzing high-resolution satellite images in Google Earth and Google Street View imagery, we settled on using the following classes: Vegetated, Harvested, Burned/Irrigated, and Other. The classes are a straightforward representation of the fundamental conditions of a farm plot and make it easier to understand.

Subset of the samples used. Imagery: Planet Labs PBC.


The vegetated class is any area that contains sugarcane. Growing sugarcane and partially harvested sugarcane were also considered part of the class. The harvested class are areas that were harvested, primarily indicated by exposed dry soil and usually appear as bright light brown in color. The Burned/Irrigated class is distinguished by dark soil due to recently burned harvested sugarcane or wet soil. Both were combined due to their similarities. The Other class represents any other class not part of the first three, including houses, trees, water, and cloud and cloud shadow. The Other class can be used as a metric for inspection as it can indicate that new structures are present in the plot or a cloud or cloud shadow was present during the analysis.

Clouds and cloud shadows were included in the “Other” class. Imagery: Planet Labs PBC.


Ideally, field status should be available daily for a detailed monitoring system. As mentioned earlier, this is slow to do manually and would be best approached using satellite imagery. Planet offers up to daily optical satellite imagery with up to eight bands at approximately 3m spatial resolution. High availability and high resolution make monitoring possible every day, which makes it suitable for monitoring sugarcane.

We developed a model with deep learning that learns the classes from a series of images from different conditions and different dates, making it applicable to use on other dates. Using a set of images captured from September 2023 from the upper half of Negros Island, we labeled 290 areas, totaling approximately 1422.7 hectares, comprising all classes for the initial model. We used ArcGIS Pro to label and train a deep learning model. We set 10% of the total samples for validation. We ran it for 120 epochs and reached an F1-score or accuracy of 86.95% for Vegetated, 88.16% for Harvested, 90.52% for Burned/Irrigated, and 88.33% for the Other class. We can continue training with more epochs and more data to achieve better accuracy; however, we will use the trained model in a test area.

Distribution of the samples, shown in red, in Negros Island. Basemap: ESRI Satellite


The Test Area

In testing the performance of models, an area that is away from where the samples are located is encouraged. For this, we selected sugarcane plantations south of the island. We chose an area near Bais City as the test site and used a PlanetScope image captured last January 28, 2024.

True-color PlanetScope image of an area in Bais City on January 28, 2024. Basemap: ESRI Satellite


To conduct vegetation monitoring, one good input is a set of field boundaries. These can be retrieved from other sources, typically from surveys or relevant agencies. However, no field boundaries are available at the test site, but we manually delineated fields based on Google Earth images that we used for the assessment. After using the model to classify the area, we clipped the output to the fields to mask out unneeded data. Note that this is for demonstration purposes only and does not represent actual field boundaries.

Manually delineated fields are shown in light blue (left), and the output of the classification (right).


Visually, we can see the fields that are still vegetated, indicating fields that are still not harvested. Harvested fields can be used as an indicator to start preparing the field for the next cropping season.

With the delineated fields, we can calculate the proportion of each class per field. This is possible by simply dividing the area of the class in the field by the total area of the field. It can be represented as a ratio or percentage. By showing the proportion of each class in a field, more information can be obtained for decision-making, such as using it as a metric to determine if a field is now ready for planting.

A sample of a stylized visualization of the result where fields with high vegetation are shown in green and the harvested are shown in red. The highlighted field is shown in cyan and the proportion of each class is shown in a pop-up window.


We also created an interactive map that is easy to use and viewable on modern desktop browsers.


How This Can Help You

Here we demonstrate the capability of satellite data and advanced technologies to provide vegetation monitoring. In this example, we used deep learning with one of the few satellites to provide near-daily imagery to provide a monitoring system for sugarcane fields. This can be used to acquire information on sugarcane fields for management purposes. Provincial or even national monitoring is possible with the large area that satellite images such as Planet capture.

While this demonstration utilized sugarcane farms, this can be converted to monitor almost any crop of interest. Talk to us about your requirements, and we can help develop a farm monitoring solution that’s right for you.
